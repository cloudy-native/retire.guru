How to Maximize Your 401(k) in Retirement

Welcome to our 401(k) Withdrawal Calculator – designed specifically for those approaching or already enjoying retirement. After years of disciplined saving, you now face an important question: how much can you safely withdraw from your 401(k) while making your savings last?

This calculator helps you determine a sustainable withdrawal strategy that complements your Social Security benefits. By analyzing your current 401(k) balance, expected investment returns during retirement, and desired lifestyle, you'll gain clarity on creating a reliable income stream for your golden years.

Explore different withdrawal rates to find the right balance between enjoying retirement today and ensuring financial security tomorrow.

Balance Projection

Given your financial goals, here's how your nest egg looks as you get older. We calculate three key scenarios: Age 62, 66 and 10 months (FRA), and 70. Your savings grow, but you're withdrawing some to maintain your monthly goals.


Here's how to read it. The chart shows your balance at the end of each year for all three age scenarios for claiming Social Security. Find which line is yours based on when you claimed Social Security, or plan to. Try adjusting your financial picture above and watch the graphs change with it. As you increase your aspirations for monthly income, the lines slope down. When the lines touch the x-axis, your savings are gone. Time to dial it back a bit.

You can change the economic assumptions that went into calculating this at the bottom of the page. For this chart, the rate of inflation is 2.4%, COLA increase 2.3%, and investment return 8%. Social Security at age 62 is $2,572.00, Full Retirement Age $3,627.00, and age 70 $4,500.00.

The Economy

Feel free to adjust the rate of inflation, the Social Security COLA (cost-of-living adjustment), and the average investment rate of return. We have good values to start with.


The average rate of inflation for the last 30 years is 2.4%. The COLA increase during that same period is 2.3%. The S&P 500 performance averages 10.3%. A more conservative portfolio of bonds averages from 4.2% to 4.7%. Common guidance suggests a portfolio of 60% stocks and 40% bonds. That would be 60% × 10.3% (stocks) + 40% × 4.5% (bonds) = 8% average annual return.
